Checked In

Improving mental health and wellness at Harvard College

Mission Statement

Checked in is dedicated to bringing about meaningful and impactful change in mental wellbeing on college campuses through increasing awareness, accessibility and emotional intelligence.

Overview

Across America, mental health among college students has reached a breaking point. Faced with unprecedented challenges from technological development and societal pressures, Gen Z students aren't receiving the support that they deserve. This has caused an explosion in the number of students struggling with their own mental wellbeing, creating a crisis that can only be halted by providing students with the knowledge and tools to improve their mental health.


User Research

Prior to joining the project the team had collected data by administering surveys to Harvard College students. The data revealed that Harvard students are struggling with mental wellbeing (i.e., loneliness, depression, anxiety). Furthermore, it was discovered that these problems were aggravated by inefficient access to, and awareness of, on-campus mental health resources, including the efficacy of existing on-campus resources.

My contributions

Challenge: How might we develop a tool to facilitate student mental wellbeing on the Harvard College campus?

My Contribution: Transform conceptual information derived from users’ needs, goals, frustrations, and motivations, into a tangible medium for testing.

I spearheaded all UX activities including but not limited to, coaching team members on UX best practices and methods, collecting data, designing (i.e., sketching, wire-framing, developing prototypes, conducting usability tests to evaluate design elements and test the assumptions we were operating under. At the same time, I developed UX strategy in order to improve the applications interaction design, overall utility and usability of our product.

I iteratively developed low fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes that were utilized during each user testing sprint. The result of each user testing sprint produced data that allowed our team to make enhancements to key features, to further understand UI navigation, and gauge usability.

The data collected from those iterations culminated in refining the aspects of the information architecture, interaction design, and usability of the application. Each insight and design recommendation extracted from the data collected was integrated into a high-fidelity prototype.

I developed the high-fidelity prototype and designed our data collection methodology used to measure comprehensive qualitative & quantitative usability metrics, further improve the interaction design, and validate the efficacy of each feature. In sum, my prototype served as a fundamental artifact used to gain acceptance to the Harvard Innovation Lab Venture Incubation Program for two consecutive terms, during the fall of 2019 and the spring of 2020.

Skills Used: Brainstorming, sketching, wire-framing, rapid prototyping, qualitative in-person usability testing, interaction design, providing guidance and support of team members including interns and stakeholders.

Time constraints: June 23rd, 2019 to Aug 7th, 2019.

Solution Generation

I leveraged the data collected from the user interviews conducted, and surveys, to develop user needs and requirements. User needs and requirements are as follow:

  1. Time constrained students need a convenient form of accessing mental health tools and support in the form of a mobile application. Everyone has a mobile phone.
  2. Given the ineffectiveness of on-campus resources, students need a tool that allows them to take charge of their own mental wellbeing. Therefore, our tool must provide research-backed features to empower students to address mental wellbeing (i.e., emotional intelligence, reflection, resources, social support).
  3. Due to the difficulty of locating on-campus resources, students need a tool to provide them with educational information about on-campus resources. This feature must act as a central forum including information on how to locate or utilize all 7 on-campus mental health resources.

Additional exploratory UX work included: completing multiple competitive analysis to identify weaknesses and strengths of existing mental health applications, administering surveys to rigorously gauge and define feature-based solutions, reviewing academic research to support the efficacy of each proposed feature (i.e., “emotional check-in, insights, social support”).


Naturally, this work culminated in wire-frames designed to meet our users’ needs and formulated the basis of feature development, and the basis of the application.

UX Activies Performed

  1. I led three UX design sprints each lasting 4-5 days
  2. I created a user flow diagram to detail each workflow a user would engage in when using the mobile application and each feature.
  3. I developed a usability testing prompt to facilitate 3 moderated in-person usability tests (n=5-10)
  4. I designed and executed two usability tests, one per iteration of the prototype I developed using Adobe Illustrator, Photoshop, and XD. After coaching team members on UX best practices I then delegated the third and final usability test which was executed by the team.
    • I ensured clear communication and involvement of each stakeholder throughout the data collection and analysis process employed during each UX sprint. This included:
    • Inviting co-founders to take notes and observe while I facilitated each usability test.
    • Instructing team members on how to perform each UX function in order to delegate tasks such as data transcription of video and audio recordings to facilitate data analysis.
    • Holding team meetings in which all members contributed to activities such as sketching, brainstorming and analysis of user research data, and data driven decision making, as well as the development of design recommendations.

These activities resulted in weekly improvements to the interaction, design, information architecture and usability of the application features. All of which culminated in a tangible, clickable, mobile friendly, high fidelity prototype.
